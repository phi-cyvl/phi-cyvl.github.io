A score you can't defend is a score you can't fund.

A public works director takes our numbers into a council meeting. Someone asks a fair question: is this still true? When the only data is last year's scan, there is no good answer. Trust in the number decides whether the work gets funded.

The idea

What if every score carried its own confidence?

Built from the scan you already paid for. No new fieldwork. No hand-measured ground truth. Each score is checked against the evidence beneath it and against how long ago the road was driven.

Verified · evidence backs the score Review · signals are mixed Rescan · evidence disagrees, or the scan is stale
What it's worth

Rescan what moved. Skip what didn't.

Confidence decays as the world changes under a scan. That decay is a map of where to look next. Send crews to the miles that carry the uncertainty, not the whole network, for a fraction of the cost, and keep the years between full scans defensible.

Real customer data · Massillon, Ohio

The confidence layer, on a real city.

Every dot is a real road segment. The score map looks healthy. Confidence, built from that same scan with no ground truth, flags which scores are least likely to still be true a year later. Then compare it to next year's scan — our ground truth — and the flagged roads are the ones that moved.

This predicts change, not a mistake in today's score. It can't see a surprise repave or a utility cut — no prior scan can — but it catches the deterioration already underway, which is most of what moves.

Cyvl already ships a deterioration curve — the expected PCI decline for each road. Toggle vs the model: red roads fell faster than the curve predicted (hidden failure — subsurface, trucks, utilities); blue roads beat it (a treatment working). Confidence predicts that deviation a year before the rescan confirms it — and every rescan feeds a sharper, per-segment curve back. That loop is the product.

The math, and why you can trust it

Confidence you can't fake.

The score can't grade its own homework, so we make it predict something it will be checked against, then score that prediction with a rule that only rewards honesty.

Each score is really a set of predictions from independent evidence: the imagery distress read, the LiDAR, the coverage, last year's condition, and the neighbors. We hold one out, predict it from the rest, and grade the prediction with a strictly proper scoring rule (the Brier score):
confidence loss = ( p − o )²  averaged over held-out checks
p = the score's predicted probability, o = what the evidence actually showed (1 or 0). The one mathematical fact that makes this trustworthy: a proper scoring rule is minimized only when p equals the true probability. You cannot lower your loss by sounding more sure than you are. Over-confidence and under-confidence both get punished.
That is why we do not use the obvious signals. "High coverage" or "the models agreed" can be gamed: in our own research a model chasing those grew more and more confident while getting less and less accurate. Proper scoring can't be gamed that way, so the confidence stays honest as the world changes. We check it with a reliability diagram: when we say 80% confident, it should be right about 80% of the time.

Three independent audits feed one number: cross-signal (do imagery, LiDAR, and coverage agree?), temporal (last year's score is a prediction of this year, absent treatment), and spatial (a segment scored unlike all its neighbors is suspect). Low on any of them means "don't over-trust, rescan first."

Method
Confidence uses a strictly proper scoring rule (Brier): minimized only when the stated probability equals the true one, so it cannot be gamed. Calibration is checked with a reliability diagram.
Proper scoring: Gneiting & Raftery, 2007.
The forecaster is composition-first: a semi-Markov process over distress states, PCI derived via ASTM D6433, grounded in 20+ years of federal LTPP / C-LTPP data and never fit to the city it forecasts. Year-0 equals the scan exactly.
