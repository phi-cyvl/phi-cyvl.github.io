Infrastructure Intelligence

A confidence score for every road.
No ground truth required.

Cities manage billions of dollars of pavement on data that is often years out of date. The score on the map might be right. It might be stale. Today there is no way to tell which.

A score you can't defend is a score you can't fund.

A public works director takes our numbers into a council meeting. Someone asks a fair question: is this still true? When the only data is last year's scan, there is no good answer. Trust in the number decides whether the work gets funded.

The idea

What if every score carried its own confidence?

Built from the scan you already paid for. No new fieldwork. No hand-measured ground truth. Each score is checked against the evidence beneath it and against how long ago the road was driven.

Verified · evidence backs the score Review · signals are mixed Rescan · evidence disagrees, or the scan is stale
What it's worth

Rescan what moved. Skip what didn't.

Confidence decays as the world changes under a scan. That decay is a map of where to look next. Send crews to the miles that carry the uncertainty, not the whole network, for a fraction of the cost, and keep the years between full scans defensible.

A few years out

Now let the rescanning drive itself.

Three ways to run the same city. A random driver. A greedy heuristic. And a swarm trained with no ground truth to re-scan exactly where the map has gone stale. Cars are mobile scanners; poles are fixed traffic-camera sensors. Watch all three, and put any one in the center.

Grey builds up as belief; red is where the belief is currently wrong. Higher IoU = a more accurate map.
