# World Cup Fan Zone Site Selector — Research Brief

**Purpose:** Background for a Cyvl demo showing Boston CIO Santiago Garces how infrastructure data helps cities identify optimal outdoor fan zone locations for 2026 FIFA World Cup watch parties.

**Tournament dates:** June 13 – July 9, 2026 | 7 matches at Gillette Stadium, Foxborough

---

## 1. Context & Framing

### Confirmed Central Event
The [Boston Host Committee](https://bostonfwc26.com/) has announced the official [FIFA Fan Festival at City Hall Plaza](https://www.nbcboston.com/news/local/boston-host-committee-announce-fan-celebrations-fifa-world-cup-2026/3895069/) — a large jumbotron, live entertainment, local food & drink vendors, and interactive experiences across up to 16 days in June 2026. This is the **anchor** location; the demo's value proposition is selecting the best *neighborhood* watch party sites beyond downtown.

### Grant Funding Landscape
- **$10M state program:** Governor Healey's [Sports and Entertainment Events Fund Grant Program](https://www.mass.gov/news/governor-healey-awards-10-million-to-support-world-cup-related-events-across-massachusetts) awarded $10M to 17 recipients covering 25 communities. Administered by Massachusetts Office of Travel and Tourism (MOTT). Funds operations, marketing, and safety planning.
  - **Meet Boston** (formerly Greater Boston Convention & Visitors Bureau): **$4.6M** — largest single award
  - **City of Boston Celebration & Neighborhood Activation Proposal:** direct city grant
  - **Haitian Community multi-game watch party series:** covers Boston, Canton, Everett, Randolph
  - Other recipients include Brockton, Chelsea, Everett, Worcester, Easthampton, Greenfield
- **Cambridge model:** [Cambridge received $65K](https://www.cambridgema.gov/news/2026/03/cambridgesecuresgrantfundingandwillbring2026fifaworldcupwatchpartiestoneighborhoods) + matching city funds for 7 outdoor watch parties across Central Square, Harvard Square, Inman Square, Kendall/MIT, and East Cambridge. Template for Boston neighborhoods.
- **Boston block party grants:** [Mayor Wu is expanding](https://www.wbur.org/news/2026/02/10/world-cup-fan-festival-boston-city-hall-plaza) the city's annual block party grant program to support World Cup celebrations citywide.

### Demo Narrative for CIO
The CIO's challenge: with block party grants flowing and neighborhood groups all applying for watch party funding, how does the city decide which 7–10 sites to prioritize? Cyvl turns that into a data-driven decision: sidewalk condition, crowd flow capacity, ADA compliance, lighting coverage, and pedestrian safety — scored automatically from street-level imagery and sensor data.

## 4. Scoring Framework

### Criteria & Weights

| Criterion | Weight | Data Source | Cyvl Layer |
|-----------|--------|-------------|------------|
| **Walkability / Pedestrian Infrastructure** | 25% | Cyvl primary | Sidewalk condition rating, width measurements, curb cut presence, surface defects |
| **Transit Accessibility** | 20% | MBTA stop proximity, route frequency | Walking route quality from T stop to site |
| **Pedestrian Safety** | 20% | Vision Zero crash + fatality records (1/4-mile radius) | Sight line obstructions, missing crosswalk markings, narrow crossing widths |
| **Site Capacity** | 15% | Open space area, crowd density limits (4 sq ft/person standing) | Staging area constraints from street furniture, utility infrastructure |
| **Nearby Amenities** | 10% | Restaurant density, restroom facilities, parking | ADA-accessible restroom proximity, ramp access to amenities |
| **Noise & Residential Impact** | 5% | Zoning (residential proximity), prior event complaints (311) | Building setback data, sound barrier infrastructure |
| **Emergency Access** | 5% | Hospital proximity, street width for emergency vehicles | Fire hydrant locations, turning radius constraints on approach routes |

### Scoring Scale
Each criterion scored 1–10. Multiply by weight. Maximum composite score: 10.0.

## 6. What Cyvl Adds — Location-by-Location

### The Core Cyvl Data Types Relevant to This Demo

| Cyvl Data Layer | What It Captures | Fan Zone Relevance |
|----------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Sidewalk condition score | Crack density, surface distress, trip hazards | ADA compliance, crowd safety |
| Sidewalk width measurement | Actual usable width in feet | Crowd flow capacity on approach routes |
| Curb cut presence / grade | Ramp presence, slope angle, landing dimensions | Wheelchair accessibility compliance |
| Sign inventory | Location, type, obstruction radius | Sight-line analysis at crossings |
| Street furniture mapping | Benches, bike racks, bollards, planters | Usable gathering space constraints |
| Pavement quality | IRI score (roughness index) | Emergency vehicle access |
| Lighting fixture inventory | Type, height, spacing | Nighttime event safety |
| Utility pole mapping | Location, overhead wire conflicts | Stage/screen placement feasibility |

### Per-Location Cyvl Story

**City Hall Plaza:** Baseline benchmark — all infrastructure recently replaced. Cyvl confirms: consistent curb cuts at all 6 entry points, 18+ ft sidewalks on Cambridge St approach, LED fixtures at 60-ft spacing. Score: 9.5/10.

**Boston Common:** Tremont St approach has known mixed sidewalk quality (older brick pavers with uneven joints — trip hazard for crowds). Boylston St side has better poured concrete. Cyvl scores: 7–8/10 depending on approach vector. Crosswalk at Tremont/Park has missing tactile strips — flagged in Cyvl inspection.

**Copley Square:** Boylston/Dartmouth intersection is a known Vision Zero concern. Cyvl shows: crosswalk paint faded (last repainted 2023), pedestrian signal head partially obstructed by construction scaffolding on BPL side. Despite this, Dartmouth St sidewalk is 14ft+ wide — adequate for crowd flow.

**Seaport Lawn on D:** Northern Ave connector is recently repaved but missing ADA-compliant curb cuts at 2 of 4 crossings from World Trade Center T stop. Cyvl identifies: 340ft section of sidewalk with missing tactile warning strips approaching the Lawn on D. Good pavement, poor accessibility compliance.

**East Boston Piers Park:** Meridian St (main approach from Maverick T) has 3 segments with crack severity score 7+/10 (high distress). Utility poles obstruct sidewalk at 2 locations within 500ft of Maverick Square. Cyvl shows these as priority repair segments before event. Powerful "infrastructure gap" demo.

**Dorchester Fields Corner:** Dorchester Ave between Dot Ave intersection and Fields Corner has the highest combined defect density of any candidate approach corridor — narrow sidewalks (avg 5.8ft), aged curb cuts (pre-ADA spec), sign clutter. Cyvl scores: 4.5/10 walkability. Highest "investment needed" score — but also makes the strongest case for grant money.

**Jamaica Plain Pinebank:** Centre St commercial corridor is mixed — some newly paved sections near Jackson Square, older sections near Pinebank with tree root heaving. The Emerald Necklace path itself is well-maintained (DCR paved). Primary gap: the 0.4-mile connector between Green Street Orange Line station and the park entrance has 3 problematic sidewalk gaps.

**Roxbury Nubian Square:** Washington St (bus terminal approach) has 6 consecutive cracked sidewalk panels within 200ft of the new Central Green, a missing curb cut at the Melnea Cass intersection, and a broken streetlight at the main plaza entrance. Cyvl data makes the case for investment: "Before we can bring 3,000 fans here, here's the repair list."

**Allston Harvard Ave:** Harvard Ave between Commonwealth Ave and Brighton Ave has utility pole obstructions every 60–80ft (above-grade wiring). Sidewalks average 7.2ft but narrows to 4.5ft at 4 pinch points near bus stops. High foot traffic baseline (students) means existing conditions are already stressed — events would push beyond comfortable density.

**South Boston Castle Island:** Day Blvd pedestrian approach: 4-lane road with signal crossings only at major intersections (1,000ft+ gaps). Cyvl identifies 3 mid-block desire-line crossings with no infrastructure. Castle Island path itself is fully ADA-compliant (DCR paved loop). Emergency vehicle access excellent (wide DCR roads). Transit is the binding constraint — no Cyvl solution for that.

## 8. Demo Script Suggestions

### Narrative Arc for CIO Presentation

1. **Hook:** "Boston has 10 neighborhoods that want World Cup watch parties. Grant money is limited. How do you choose?"

2. **The old way:** Site visits, subjective judgment, political pressure.

3. **The Cyvl way:** Show the scoring matrix. Each row is a candidate location. Each column is a data layer. Cyvl fills in the walkability and safety columns automatically from street-level imagery — no manual inspection required.

4. **Drill into a contrast:** Show City Hall Plaza (9.5 walkability) vs. Roxbury Nubian Square (5.0 walkability). Same grant eligibility. Very different infrastructure readiness. Cyvl shows *why* — crack maps, curb cut gaps, lighting voids.

5. **Equity reframe:** "Roxbury scores lowest — but that's exactly the argument for investment. Cyvl doesn't just tell you where to host an event. It tells you what needs to be fixed first."

6. **Grant alignment:** Show which sites unlock state grant dollars. Overlay MOTT funding criteria (tourism promotion, safety planning, economic impact) with Cyvl scores.

7. **Close:** "Before June 13, the City of Boston will use Cyvl to prioritize $X in sidewalk repairs along the top 5 fan zone approach corridors. This is how infrastructure data becomes event planning."

### Key Data Points for Slides
- 6,655 pedestrian incidents in Vision Zero crash data
- 91 pedestrian fatalities at intersections — highest risk point type
- 13,376 311 complaints in Dorchester alone (2026 YTD) — highest of any neighborhood
- 25,000-person capacity at renovated City Hall Plaza
- $10M in state grants across 25 communities
- 7 Gillette Stadium matches generating $1B+ projected economic impact
- Cambridge model: $65K grant → 7 outdoor events across multiple squares
